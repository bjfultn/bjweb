Research

Exoplanet Demographics

With large samples of planets we can begin to measure the statistical properties of the exoplanet population. I am interested in two big-picture questions about exoplanets:

  • What percentage of stars like our sun host exoplanets?
  • What is the overall distribution of exoplanet masses and sizes?

I discovered that planets smaller than Neptune tend to come in one of two distinct size classes. This observation has significant implications for planet formation theories. The size class in which a planet falls also tells us about that planet's composition and can allow us to distinguish between rocky super-Earths, and gaseous mini-Neptunes.

My ongoing APF-50 survey is designed to perform a similar measurement but by measuring planet masses instead of sizes. We are systematically and autonomously surveying a sample of bright, nearby stars using the APF. I hope to measure the masses of a statistically useful sample of nearby planets in order to infer the underlying distribution of planet masses in the local part of the galaxy.

Robotic Telescopes

Telescope automation is the way forward in observational astronomy. Cutting edge research requires massive datasets that can not be collected by humans manually driving telescopes from star to star. Advances in computing and weather monitoring have allowed software to take the night shift, freeing up more time for astronomers to analyze and interpret the data. I wrote much of the high-level automation software that runs the Automated Planet Telescope (APF) at Lick Observatory. This telescope is now operating autonomously every clear night, surveying bright, nearby stars for Earth-like planets.

Doppler Spectroscopy

I am primarily interested in measuring the masses, locations, and frequency of nearby exoplanets. I use high-resolution optical spectrographs to precisely measure the Doppler shifts stars as they wobble under the gravitational influence of orbiting planets.
